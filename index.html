Interactive Lesson Plan: The Giver

The Giver: Interactive Plan

Session 1 Session 2 Session 3

Lesson Plan for Korean EFL High School Students

This interactive plan translates the three-session guide for teaching "The Giver" into a dynamic experience. The goal is to enhance reading comprehension, critical thinking, and personal connection to the novel's themes of individuality, memory, choice, and societal control.

Session 1: Introduction & Setting the Stage

Chapters 1-5

Objectives

  • Identify key characters and the initial setting of Jonas's community.
  • Understand the concept of "Sameness" and its implications.
  • Begin to reflect on their own ideas of a perfect society.
  • Expand vocabulary related to the initial chapters.

Vocabulary Flashcards

Click on each card to reveal its meaning. This activity helps students actively learn and recall new terms from the first five chapters.

Apprehensive

Anxious or fearful that something bad will happen.

Chastisement

The act of scolding or punishing someone.

Release

In the community, a term for death, which is hidden from citizens.

Ceremony of Twelve

A major event where 12-year-olds are assigned their lifelong jobs.

Interactive Discussion Points

Click each question to expand it. This allows for a focused classroom discussion, guiding students through the key concepts of the initial chapters.

Encourage students to think about the order, predictability, and lack of emotion. Prompt them with ideas like "sameness," family units, and strict rules. Contrast this with their own society.

At this stage, let students predict freely. Common guesses might be banishment, being sent to another community, or a special celebration. This builds suspense for the later reveal.

Session 2: Deepening Understanding & Conflict

Chapters 6-15

Objectives

  • Analyze the significance of the Ceremony of Twelve and "Assignments."
  • Explore themes of choice, emotion, and the burden of memory.
  • Empathize with Jonas's experiences as the new Receiver.
  • Connect the idea of memory to their own personal experiences.

Personal Connection: The Weight of Memory

Jonas receives both joyful and painful memories, highlighting the core themes of the novel. This chart visualizes the key concepts that students are encouraged to reflect upon in the "Memory I Cherish/Erase" activity, showing the balance between positive and negative aspects of a full life.

Interactive Discussion Points

Expand the questions to explore the central conflicts and character developments in this section of the book.

Discuss the high honor, but also the isolation and pain associated with it. Talk about the rules he receives, especially the one allowing him to lie. How does this separate him from everyone else?

Guide students to understand that the community gave up deep emotions, choice, and color to gain safety and predictability. They avoided pain, but also lost true joy. Ask students if they think this is a fair trade.

Session 3: Climax, Resolution & Application

Chapters 16-End

Objectives

  • Analyze the climax and resolution of the novel.
  • Evaluate Jonas's final decision and its implications.
  • Synthesize personal learning and apply themes to real-world scenarios.
  • Express understanding through creative reflection.

Debate: "Should Jonas Have Left?"

This activity encourages critical thinking and argumentation. Click the buttons below to reveal the core arguments for each side of the debate, providing a clear framework for discussion.

FOR Leaving
  • He chose freedom over safety.
  • He was saving Gabe's life.
  • He wanted to give memories (and feelings) back to the community.
  • He could no longer live a lie.
AGAINST Leaving
  • He abandoned his family and friends.
  • The memories could cause chaos and pain for the community.
  • He faced unknown, potentially lethal dangers.
  • Was it his right to make that choice for everyone?

Final Discussion: The Ending

The novel's ambiguous ending is a powerful point for discussion. Use these questions to guide a final conversation about its meaning.

Explore the two main interpretations: 1) Jonas and Gabe find a real, welcoming village ('Elsewhere'). 2) Jonas is succumbing to hypothermia and the final scene is a hallucination or memory as he dies. There is no right answer; encourage students to support their opinion with evidence from the text.
